The Four Pillars

What a strong
estate plan
actually covers

A complete estate plan is built on four interconnected foundations. Miss one and the whole structure is exposed. Together, they ensure your wishes are carried out without conflict, delay, or unnecessary taxation.

01
Inventory
A clear, current inventory of your accounts, titled assets, insurance policies, and digital assets prevents delays and ensures nothing is overlooked during estate settlement. Missing assets are a common and costly problem — often worth tens of thousands of dollars that heirs simply never know to claim.
02
Continuity
Durable powers of attorney and healthcare directives protect your ability to have decisions made on your behalf if you become incapacitated. Without them, courts may appoint a guardian — a lengthy, expensive process that removes control from your family at the worst possible moment.
03
Liquidity
Estate taxes, final expenses, and family needs require immediate cash. Without a liquidity plan, estates are forced to sell investments, real estate, or business interests — often at a discount — to satisfy obligations. Life insurance is frequently the most efficient source of estate liquidity.
04
Governance
Trusts, trustees, and family governance structures ensure clarity about roles and decision-making. Ambiguous instructions and disputed authority are the leading causes of family conflict during estate settlement. Clear governance prevents this and ensures consistent execution of your wishes across generations.
Core Documents

Four documents
every plan requires

01
Last Will & Testament
Foundation
Your will directs how your assets are distributed and names guardians for minor children. Without one, state intestacy laws decide — and the state's default may bear no resemblance to your wishes. A will must be properly signed and witnessed to be valid.
02
Revocable Living Trust
Privacy & Probate Avoidance
A revocable trust holds assets during your lifetime and transfers them to beneficiaries without probate — privately, faster, and without court involvement. Unlike a will, a trust is not a public record. Most clients with meaningful assets benefit from a funded revocable trust.
03
Durable Power of Attorney
Incapacity Planning
A POA authorizes a trusted person to manage your financial affairs if you are unable to. Without one, banks and institutions will freeze accounts until a court appoints a conservator. This is one of the simplest and most important documents anyone can have.
04
Healthcare Directives
Medical Decision-Making
A healthcare POA and living will document your medical wishes and designate who can make healthcare decisions on your behalf. Without these, family members may disagree — or courts may intervene — during medical emergencies when clarity matters most.
Planning Update — 2026
The sunset was prevented — the $15M exemption is now permanent
The One Big Beautiful Bill Act, signed July 4, 2025, permanently eliminated the TCJA sunset provision. Beginning January 1, 2026, the federal estate and gift tax exemption rises to $15 million per individual ($30 million for married couples) — and is indexed for inflation going forward. The feared reversion to ~$7 million did not occur. While the urgency of the year-end deadline has passed, strategic estate planning remains essential. Trust structures, lifetime gifting, and multi-generational planning are still among the most powerful tools available — and the right time to review your plan is now, not when circumstances force it.

Questions

Estate planning
answered.

Every client comes to estate planning with different circumstances and different concerns. These are the questions we encounter most often — answered plainly, without jargon.

Your situation will always call for a personal conversation.

A will alone requires your estate to pass through probate — a public court process that can take months or years and costs 3–8% of the estate's value in fees. A funded revocable trust avoids probate entirely, transfers assets immediately and privately, and provides continuity of management if you become incapacitated. For most clients with property in multiple states or significant assets, a trust is the superior choice.

Dying intestate (without a will or trust) means state law decides who receives your assets and who raises your children. The state's formula is rigid and may direct assets to relatives you would not have chosen, while excluding partners or others you would have prioritized. The process is public, can take years, and frequently generates family conflict — all of which are completely avoidable with even a basic plan in place.

At minimum, review your estate plan every 3–5 years and after any major life event: marriage, divorce, birth of a child, death of a named beneficiary or trustee, significant change in wealth, or major tax law change. Many clients discover that beneficiary designations on IRAs and life insurance policies are years or decades out of date — sometimes naming ex-spouses or deceased individuals. These designations override your will entirely.

Portability allows a surviving spouse to use any unused portion of their deceased spouse's estate tax exemption. In 2026, a married couple can effectively shield up to $30M from federal estate tax — but portability is not automatic. A federal estate tax return (Form 706) must be filed within 9 months of the first spouse's death, even if no tax is owed. Missing this deadline permanently forfeits the unused exemption.

Life insurance death benefits are income-tax-free, but they are included in your estate for estate tax purposes if you own the policy. An Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust (ILIT) removes the policy from your taxable estate while using the death benefit to provide liquidity for estate taxes — allowing heirs to receive the full value of other assets rather than being forced to sell them. This is one of the most cost-efficient estate planning strategies available to taxable estates.
